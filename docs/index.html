Stop unseen cyberattacks without sharing your network data.

Signature-based tools only catch attacks they've already seen, so a brand-new one slips straight past. The model

Connection logs in, suspicion scores out.

The detector is built from one core idea, the autoencoder, plus one twist, the graph.

An autoencoder is a model with two halves. The encoder squeezes the input down to a tiny summary. The decoder rebuilds the input from that summary alone. Train it on normal traffic and it becomes an expert at rebuilding normal traffic.

Now put it on a graph.

STEP 1 / 5 Draw the graph. Take 1,000 connection records and turn them into a directed graph: each device a dot, each connection an arrow from sender to receiver.
What it needs

Only seven fields needed per connection.

The same connection metadata most routers already export:

duration how long it lasted packets how many crossed bytes how much data moved minimum TTL roughly how far away the sender is protocol the language spoken: TCP, UDP… source port which service sent it dest port what it's for: web, mail, DNS…

Federated learning

Train together. Keep your data private.

Instead of moving sensitive data to the model, we move the model to the data. This keeps private network logs entirely local while building a powerful, shared defense:

  • Distribute: A coordinator sends the global model to each organization.
  • Train: Each organization trains the model locally using its own private traffic.
  • Aggregate: They send back only what the model learned—never the raw network logs.

Results · six insights

What the experiments show.

Six insights, split by the two ideas from the top: first the graph autoencoder on its own, then what federated training adds.

Approach 1 · Graph autoencoder

What a single, centralised detector can — and can't — do.

01 The trade-off

The Balance Between Threat Detection and False Alarms.

Four detectors, each trained and tested on its own network. Every detector trades the same two things off: attacks caught against normal traffic wrongly flagged.

Attacks caught vs false alarms, per network.

BoT-IoT catches 96% of attacks but wrongly flags 41% of its normal traffic; UNSW almost never false-alarms 5% but misses 30% of attacks.
02 The domain gap

A model trained on one network fails on other networks.

Take each detector out of the network it was trained on and test it on the other three. It collapses: it learned its own network's habits, not a universal notion of normal traffic.

The score used here: ROC-AUC. ROC-AUC grades how well a detector ranks connections from least to most suspicious — the more cleanly the attacks end up at the suspicious end, the higher the score:

03 Negative transfer

Simply pooling everyone's data makes a worse model.

The obvious fix — merge all four datasets and train one big model — backfires. Four very different "normals" blur into a vague average that fits nobody. This is negative transfer.

No sharing vs one big pot.

The pooled model scores 10 points below not sharing at all — despite having everyone's raw data.
04 Personalisation

Collaborative training thrives when networks retain a personalized layer.

By sharing the core model but keeping a localized decoder for each network, the system adapts to individual environments better.

One personal layer flips pooling from worst to best.

Sharing plus a personal layer beats both extremes — but it still needs all the raw data in one place.

Best of eight sharing/personalisation recipes tested; the other six fall in between.

Approach 2 · Federated learning

Train together without pooling the data — and do even better.

05 Federated learning

Federated training beats the pooled model — with zero data shared.

The model travels, the data never does.

Three ways to federate, from sharing everything to keeping a personal part.

Keeping just the scaling settings local (FedBN) wins — beating even the best centralised model, which pools everyone's raw data in one place — and halves the false alarms of every alternative, down to 8.8%.

Averages over three training runs. An alternative averaging rule (FedProx) was also tested and never beat plain averaging.

06 Generalisation

Models trained together transfer better to brand-new networks.

The hardest test: hold one network out entirely, train on the other three, then deploy on the unseen one.

Trained on three networks beats trained on one — every time.

Three teachers beat one on every unseen network (up to +31.6%) — though a newcomer still needs local tuning to reach the ceiling (◆).
Privacy mechanisms

Masking is free. Noise is not.

The two privacy options from the animation, measured. Masking the updates (SecAgg+) costs nothing. Adding noise for a provable guarantee (differential privacy) costs everything.

Masking the updates (SecAgg+): on by default. Switching the masks on left detection essentially unchanged on all four networks — the masks cancel out exactly in the coordinator's average, so the model that comes out is the same one. Full protection against a curious coordinator, at no cost.

Turn the privacy dial up, and detection collapses.

negative result

Each dot is one noise setting, from barely any (left) to a strong, provable guarantee (right).

Deployability

Fast enough to run on commodity CPUs.

Benchmarked on the 1.22M-flow UNSW holdout. The 323K-parameter model is so light that on a GPU the bottleneck is building the graphs, not running the network, and even CPU-only clears 90,000 flows per second inside 4 GB of RAM.
